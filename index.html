What is Accessibility?

Accessibility refers to the design of products, devices, services, or environments for people who experience disabilities. The concept of accessible design and practice of accessible development ensures both "direct access" (i.e. unassisted) and "indirect access" meaning compatibility with a person's assistive technology (for example, computer screen readers[1]).[2]

Nearly 1 in 5 People Have a Disability in the U.S., Census Bureau Reports[3]

About 56.7 million people — 19 percent of the population — had a disability in 2010, according to a broad definition of disability, with more than half of them reporting the disability was severe, according to a comprehensive report on this population released today by the U.S. Census Bureau.

[In 2012,] the report shows that 41 percent of those age 21 to 64 with any disability were employed, compared with 79 percent of those with no disability.

How Do We Acchieve a11y across the board?

ECMAScript can help if we manage the DOM properly. Let's look at Ember, for example:

Ember.View.extend ({ tagName: 'section' })

In this example, we can prevent class-laden <div />s that aren't recognized by standards-compliant browsers. We can do so without supurflous class names as well in our components.

But… I Need My Component to Do A Thing!

With enough planning, we can build our components better. Using the cascade available in CSS, we can structure our HTML to conform to standards. Standards compliance will allow us to build proper components/modules that can be reused more easily.

If we use the DOM as an outline, we can refrain from superfluous classnames; we can use HTML nodes to create the outline that assist screen readers, for example.

What do ".left" or ".right" classnames actually mean, especially if you don't have sight? Nesting navigation in a <nav /> node, for example, will allow screen readers to express intent. If we focus on an assumption, we prevent one-out-of-five people from using our application.

Browsers don't care about classnames.
